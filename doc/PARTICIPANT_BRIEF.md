# Before You Review: A Brief for SME Reviewers

If you read one page before joining a session, make it this one. Five minutes here saves an hour in the room.

## You're not labeling data

This session is not a labeling exercise. You're defining what *quality means* in your domain — your observations become rubric criteria, and your judgement becomes an automated judge that scores AI responses the way you would. The judge literally learns from what you write.

So write your rationales as if you're teaching a new colleague how to evaluate this work. "Bad answer" teaches nothing; "the answer skipped the verification step our process requires before any account change" teaches everything.

## What a useful observation looks like

The most valuable observations name *why* something is good or bad, in terms specific enough that someone else could apply them to a different example. A few shapes worth knowing:

| Shape | Example |
|-------|---------|
| **Recurring pattern** | "Responses keep citing the policy document without quoting the clause that applies." |
| **Boundary condition** | "This was good — but it would have been better if it mentioned X." (You just defined the line between a 4 and a 5.) |
| **Edge case** | "Fine for a routine request, but if the customer asked this mid-incident the same answer would be wrong." |
| **Failure mode** | "It recommended an action this user isn't licensed to take." |

"This was good but would have been better if..." sentences are gold — say them even when they feel like nitpicking. They're exactly how the group finds where quality levels divide.

## Score each criterion on its own

When the session reaches scoring, you'll rate responses against several criteria — and **each criterion is its own measurement**. A response that fails on truthfulness can still deserve a high score for tone or completeness.

The common mistake is letting one failure drag every other score down ("it got the facts wrong, so everything is a fail"). That feels rigorous, but it creates artificial disagreement with reviewers who scored each dimension on its own merits — and that phantom disagreement miscalibrates the judge. If the response fails everything, score it that way because each criterion independently failed, not because one did.

## The follow-up questions are steerable

After your first take on an example, the application asks follow-up questions. They're suggestions, not a script — if a question isn't germane to what you actually noticed, say so and describe what you observed instead. The next questions adapt to you. Don't spend your energy dutifully answering a beside-the-point question.

## Disagreement is the point

If another reviewer scored something differently, that's not friction to smooth over — contested examples are precisely where the group discovers what "good" really means, and they're the raw material the rubric is built from. State your view plainly and let the discussion do its work.

## Come fresh

Expect to review around 4–5 examples, each with a rating, written feedback, and a few follow-up questions. It's genuinely effortful thinking — about an hour of real concentration. Treat it like the most demanding meeting of your day, not something to do on a second monitor.
